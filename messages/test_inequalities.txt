## WHY THEY WORK...

Magic? Not really.  More like 3000 or 4000 years of people struggling
to understand mathematics...

For details on why the continued fractions work, find an elementary
treatise on continued fractions.  Wikipedia and MathWorld would be good
places to start.

In addition, you should spend some time on the "division algorithm" and
the "Euclidean GCD algorithm".

    The division algorithm (or "division lemma") involves quotients and
    remainders -- it is not the same as "long division".  It is usually
    stated as a lemma -- the underlying algorithm involves repeated
    subtraction and is implied in the proof.  A good source is Euclid's
    Elements.

    Long division is painful and totally pointless.  But there is an
    alternative and no calculator is needed.  

    If you want to do arithmetic by hand, consider Egyptian multiplication
    and division (also known as Russian peasant multiplication and division).
    The same algorithm is used for both operations -- the difference is in
    how the associated tables are used.  The prerequisites are addition,
    subtraction, doubling, and halving. (Doubling or multiplying by two is
    known as duplication.  Halving or dividing by two is sometimes called
    mediation.)

    The Euclidean GCD algorithm uses the division algorithm repeatedly
    in order to find a greatest common factor.  It does not involve
    factoring.  (Factoring is a terrible way to find a GCD unless
    you are restricted to very small numbers.)  The Euclidean GCD
    algorithm (typically referred to as the Euclidean algorithm) is
    also found, with proof, in Euclid's Elements.

    If you can add and subtract, then using Egyptian division and
    the Euclidean algorithm, you should have no trouble finding the
    GCD of any pair of four or five digit numbers by hand.  (You might
    need to learn how to divide by two, but that is not hard.)

Notes on the last continued fraction...  If we cut it off after the fifth
term, we have:
    [3; 7,15,1,292]
Notice the estimates that come up:
    n       convergent                              error
   ===     =====================================   ======================
    1       [3;] = 3                                ~ 0.14
    2       [3; 7] = 22/7                           ~ 0.001 3
    3       [3; 7,15] = 333/106                     ~ 0.000 083
    4       [3; 7,15,1] = 355/113                   ~ 0.000 000 27
    5       [3; 7,15,1,292] = 103993/33102          ~ 0.000 000 000 58
    6       [3; 7,15,1,292,1] = 104348/33215        ~ 0.000 000 000 33

With the next few terms (ones), the magnitude of the error doesn't change
dramatically.  Note also the number of positions in the denominator for
each convergent:
    n       convergent                              width (denominator)
   ===     =====================================   ======================
    1       [3;] = 3                                1
    2       [3; 7] = 22/7                           1
    3       [3; 7,15] = 333/106                     3
    4       [3; 7,15,1] = 355/113                   3
    5       [3; 7,15,1,292] = 103993/33102          5
    6       [3; 7,15,1,292,1] = 104348/33215        5

We can view the number of places in the denominator as a cost of using an
estimate, the number of iterations (n) as a cost of finding the estimate,
and the error, or more precisely its logarithm of its absolute value, as
a measure of precision.  The logarithm of the absolute value can simply be
replace by the exponent in scientific notation.

        A              B             C          D        E
    complexity    convergent     precision    width    digits
   ============  ============== ===========  =======  =======
        1         3                 -1          1        1
        2         22/7              -3          1        3
        3         333/106           -5          3        6
        4         355/113           -7          3        6
        5         103993/33102      -10         5       11
        6         104348/33215      -10         5       11

The fourth entry [3;7,15,1]=355/113 is the best we can do with a three-digit
denominator -- it gives a precision of -7, i.e., an absolute of less than
10⁻⁶.  We cannot do any better with a four-digit denominator!

The first improvement after 355/113 comes with a five-digit denominator,
namely [3;7,15,1,292]=103993/33102, which yields a precision of -10
(absolute error less than 10⁻⁹) but, instead of remembering two
three-digit numbers, we need to recall a six-digit number and a five
digit number, for a total of eleven digits.

The first column is the number of iterations need to turn the continued
fraction into an ordinary fraction.  This is summarized in the following
table:

    n    q          A            B
   === =====   ==========   ==========
   -1                  0            1
    0                  1            0           RECURRENCE RELATIONS
    1    3             3            1      A[n] = q[n] A[n-1] + A[n-2]
    2    7            22            7      B[n] = q[n] B[n-1] + B[n-2]
    3   15           333          106
    4    1           355          113
    5  292       103,993       33,102
    6    1       104,348       33,215

In each row, the absolute error in the estimate A/B decreases; but the
rate of decrease depends largely on the value of the quotient term q.
Large values of q guarantee significant jumps in precision.

Some notes on e:

The regular continued fraction for e has a simple pattern even though it is
not periodic:

    [2; 1,2, 1,1,4, 1,1,6, 1,1,8, 1,1,10, ...]

Following two ones is the next consecutive even number.  The square root of
e also has an interesting pattern:

    [1; 1, 1,1,5, 1,1,9, 1,1,13, 1,1,17, 1,1,21, ...]
